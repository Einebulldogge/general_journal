Introduction:

It can be argued that not even the Mariana Trench reaches depths greater than that of the FASB’s codification. If James Cameron ever became an accountant, he would most likely confirm this. While having more rules clearly makes it harder for companies to commit fraud or misrepresentation, many latent consequences can arise from an intricate rules system. One of these consequences can be seen within the topic of disclosures.


GAAP Background on Disclosures:

Guidance on what, when, and how to disclose information to company stakeholders in annual and interim reports is sprinkled in hundreds of different sections within the codification. Even though there are independent publishers that have provided condensed versions of guidance that only focus on disclosures, it is nearly impossible to learn all that must be disclosed. Now, this is to no fault of the FASB; the intricacies of the business world demand high levels of specificity from regulating authorities. Levels of specificity that cannot be attained with a simple disclosure policy. In addition, accountants often specialize in certain areas such as ‘Fixed Assets’ or ‘Revenue Recognition’, reducing the need to memorize each and every disclosure requirement. For these reasons, the guidance on disclosures will most likely become only more extensive in the future.


What Disclosures Don't Reveal:

So, if the accountants know what their company needs to disclose, what’s the problem? The problem does not lie internally, but externally. The readers of financial statements are not all accountants. Some are passive investors, some are finance professionals, and so on. This means that many users of the financial information that public companies release will not know which disclosures have been included voluntarily or involuntarily. This fact conflicts with two of the four main enhancing qualitative characteristics of the FASB’s conceptual framework—‘understandability’ and ‘comparability’.


Enhancing Disclosures:

Not all complex problems require complex solutions. One easily implementable solution would be to require all companies to leave an ‘[I]’, or ‘[V]’ next to each disclosure to denote whether the following information/data was involuntary or voluntary. Auditors could then test the judgment of the accounting team in determining where each symbol should be used.

While all of this may seem trivial, knowing where a company chooses to insert more voluntary disclosures can help the user quickly decide the extent to which they want to analyze that piece of information. Furthermore, the user will be better positioned to compare the disclosures of companies in similar industries. As increased competition and innovative technologies continue to transform the business landscape, the investors and creditors deserve greater transparency. Think about it; are the current requirements truly ‘full disclosure’?
