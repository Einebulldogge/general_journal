Creative Accounting—Meet the “Normalcy Score” (5 min.)

.

—Written by Chris McFarland
    July 1, 2025
    Commentary last added on May 19, 2026

Introduction:

There’s nothing illegal about working within the conceptual framework and codification established by the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB); all public companies participate in creative accounting. Furthermore, the ability to be creative is paramount to the existence of accountants in large-cap companies who have heavily automated accounting systems. But, much like everything else, each occurrence lies on a spectrum of distortion.


Defining Distortion:

I use the word “distortion” because it can be used for both describing and measuring the effect of creative accounting. In this case, distortion refers to a change from what an independent certified public accountant (CPA) would have done in the same situation. As for the measurement, that would be determined by the degree of distortion, which could be in the form of quantifiable or qualitative differences between the creative act and what would be expected from an independent CPA.

——That sounds like a lot of work, going into every company and replicating what a CPA would do in each situation!

It’s more than a lot of work; it’s a ton of work!


Current Regulations Around Distortions:

——Isn’t that what auditors do, though?

At this moment in time, auditors mainly test to see if the company’s financial statements are meeting the standards set by the FASB, and if their internal controls are strong enough to thwart fraud from occurring. Even still, if the accounting adheres to Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) and the company can show that they actively discourage fraud through their systems and guidelines, they can receive an unqualified opinion. The problem with this is, while GAAP has no shortage of rules, it also offers many levers for company leaders to pull. Even though many of these levers are well-documented, to fully understand the creative act, one must fully understand the motive.

Remember the aim of accounting—to provide useful information to investors and creditors that can be used in decision-making. While creditors dive deep into the financials of their debtors, investors, many of whom are passively trying to accumulate enough wealth to comfortably retire, don’t have the ability or access to analyze the distortion of every company’s accounting practices that they have equity in. Should the accounting world settle for this? I argue we should not.


A Possible Solution:

My solution would be to advance the current auditing practices used in the US so that every publicly traded company’s creative accounting techniques can be analyzed and placed on a spectrum from similar to what a CPA would do, to severely different than what a CPA would do.

Whenever we measure anything, our goal is to be able to conceptualize the very thing we are measuring. Comparing the degree of difference between what was done and what would normally have been done will allow us to move past the four opinions of auditors and enter into a new world that incentivizes not only honesty, but “normalcy”.

——That sounds kind of boring, though, where’s the fun in that?

Believe me, “normal” is confusing enough in the accounting world. That is because, for each situation, there will often be several ways to account for it the way a CPA would. Additionally, companies already have to justify why they make certain accounting changes, such as changes in accounting methods, estimates, and reporting entities. The only difference with what I am proposing is that companies would be exposed for how far away their practices are from normal. Just like how the public can see how “green” a company is, the public will see how “normal” a company’s financials are. This could be in the form of a score from 1-100, with 100 being perfectly normal.


Conclusion:

This score may aid in thawing the ice that coats the slippery slope of fraud. History shows that there is a fine line between accounting ingenuity and fraud, and the closer motivated leaders get to that line, the more likely they are to cross it at some point down the road. That’s me being optimistic, but at the minimum, a normalcy score would lead to a more well-informed investor/creditor community. The game that corporate accountants and managers play with auditors will still go on; it will simply be measured and written into the opinion of the independent auditor. Who knows, maybe they will even make a new ETF—Companies with Honest and Normal Accounting Practices (NORM).
