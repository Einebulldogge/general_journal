Introduction:

External auditors and forensic accountants pride themselves on being able to outsmart the dubious accounting techniques that desperate or greedy companies employ. They review samples of invoices, document the internal controls, and analyze the corporate structure of consolidated entities in search of misrepresentations, embezzlement, and fraud. But, as accounting information systems (AISs) become increasingly guided by artificial intelligence (AI), external auditors will have a difficult time deciphering what is real and what is not.

Real World Example:

At the moment, most accounting systems are only non-AI-automated, and although they have been shown to increase levels of accuracy, they have presented significant challenges for auditors. For instance, Charles Schwab’s auditor, Deloitte, identified a critical audit matter (CAM) with Schwab’s automated revenue recognition system. Their asset management and administration fees, along with their commissions from trading revenue, which account for nearly $9 billion in revenue, were said to be “complex and challenging”, needing “professionals with expertise in information technology” to audit (Charles Schwab Form 10K, 2024).

This is not to say Schwab has done anything wrong. As a leader in the financial services industry, the sheer volume of transactions has demanded such recognition strategies. I only use this example to emphasize the risks that exist even with non-AI-automated systems. In the following months and years, the increasingly complex business environment will likely warrant using AI-assisted recognition systems. How will external auditors combat this inevitable shift? What do AI-proof tests look like?


Auditing AI's Events and Reports:

First, we have to understand how companies will use AI for their reporting. I predict that large fintech companies will be the first ones to implement AI into their reporting of the following economic events: fair-market adjustments, stable-coin transactions, and various hedging activities. These types of events have three similar characteristics. They occur continuously, they are largely immaterial on an individual basis (micro-transactions), and they involve the most math. These are just a few examples, but the most interesting uses will probably be for economic events that do not even exist yet! For example, “quarterly governmental mandatory equity issuance allowance” (I just made that up).

——Why would a company use AI for reporting if non-AI-automated systems work just fine?

Fair question. There are two main reasons. I already touched on the first reason earlier, which is that the complexity of the business environment is increasing, especially for companies with high market caps in the investments sector, or ones where investments make up more than 10% of their total assets. In these situations, companies can reduce their costs of reporting by not only having an AI agent make decisions on how to structure the transaction/event, but then report that event as well. It does not take much imagination to wonder how fraud could leak into a system like this. Companies can train their AI-automated systems to show auditors sample data that never happened. Collusion could occur with multiple AI systems from different companies. What looks like a way to enhance reporting precision may, in fact, rejuvenate motivations to commit fraud that mimic those of the “.com” era.

——Can’t we just ban these types of systems then?

External auditors may not even know if AI is being used. These systems could be working in the background, masquerading as a regular, non-AI-automated system. Whistleblowers could help in this case, but in a tight job market, whistleblowing isn’t too popular.

——So what can auditors do?

One approach would be to expand the fieldwork. Since the COVID-19 pandemic, audits have become increasingly virtual. This method has proved to be very successful from 2020-2025, but even with AI enhancements on the external auditors' side, it will be no match for the most creative managers.

If transparency is still not attainable through continuous field-work efforts, external auditors will be forced to measure predictive modeling estimates against the sample data provided to them. For instance, the auditors could model how interest rates would affect gains and losses on hedging instruments during a specific period. Then, they compare their models to what the audited company is reporting. Finally, they disclose the degree to which the company’s reporting aligns with the range of estimates in the CAM. Adding quantitative insights to CAMs would be an effective way to deter fraud in these cases.


Moving Forward:

In conclusion, fraud and innovation share a common thread—they never sleep. AI is here to stay, and as companies use it to streamline all aspects of their business, including micro-transaction decision-making and reporting, auditors must always adapt. Even if tomorrow’s “faithful representation” doesn’t quite look the same as today’s, that’s okay. The goal of auditing has never been about perfection; it is about protection. Protecting stakeholders from being misled by fraud and misrepresentation is the goal. I believe increasing fieldwork and using market-data-driven financial models to estimate reporting ranges will be key to ensuring this goal is met in the years to come.
