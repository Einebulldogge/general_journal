Introduction:

The President of the United States has urged the SEC to reconsider their rule of mandatory reporting every 90 days for publicly traded companies (Yi, 2025). This regulation, dating back to the year 1970, has required one audited, annual report and three, unaudited, quarterly reports to be filed with the SEC each fiscal year (SEC, 2025). Soon, the SEC will decide on whether to allow public companies to report semi-annual earnings reports, meaning two reports every six months.

Background:

Where do other parts of the world stand? Much like accounting standards, there is some uniformity abroad, but nothing truly concrete. The European Union, China, and Japan largely do not make their publicly traded companies report earnings four times a year, but they vary in their discretion. India is in the same boat as the US, currently mandating that companies report on a quarterly basis (Short, 2022).

Furthermore, it is important to recognize that there are even some publicly traded companies in the US that are given an exception to the current regulation. Foreign private issuers—foreign-based companies listed on US stock exchanges that have majority voting rights located outside the US are allowed to report earnings on a semi-annual basis (“What is a Foreign Private Issuer (FPI)?”, n.d.).


The US's Current Position:

So, where does this leave us? It appears likely that the US will follow the current global trend. For most publicly traded companies, the SEC will likely grant them the option to report semi-annually. In a CNBC interview, newly appointed chair of the SEC, Paul Atkins, says he embraces the President’s view, and if a switch to reporting every six months were made, that investors should dictate whether companies should report more often (Yi, 2025).

While the ‘Father of Economics’, Adam Smith, and popular opinion would most likely agree with Atkins' comments, the story does not end there. Will only one earnings report still have to be audited, or will both reports now be audited? Will there still be earnings calls on a quarterly basis? Under what type of procedure will investors have the option to request additional reporting? So many questions remain, with the most important ones being how markets will digest such a massive change and how this will affect the economy as a whole.


Implications for Auditing:

Many other articles discuss the potential consequences of this change, but for the purposes of this article, I want to discuss the first question I posed, which pertains to the role auditing will play in a world with only two reports per fiscal year.

First off, I believe that a switch to semi-annual reporting would benefit companies and both retail and institutional investors. In my opinion, quarterly reporting mimics more of an attendance grade rather than a report card. Having a greater number of operating cycles between each reporting date will allow companies to focus more on innovation and much-needed risk-taking in this unprecedented period of technological change. Investors will still have plenty of information to trade on, including 8-K filings, earnings calls, important economic data from institutions such as the Fed and BLS, and market trends.

However, I do also believe that if we decide to reduce the quantity of our reports, we should increase the quality of our reports. How do we increase the quality of our reports? Auditing! Instead of only the annual report being audited, I advocate that the SEC should require both semi-annual reports to be audited by an independent auditor.

——But that defeats the point. All the cost and time savings will be gone! We’re trying to make more companies go public, not less!

That is a fair point. Having a healthy economy depends on having a steady flow of companies tapping into the U.S. equity markets. However, the cost of two audited financial reports will not be (Cost of Form 10-K * 2). There will be a smaller time span to audit and, therefore, a more marginal increase in cost overall rather than a doubling. Additionally, having one report remain unaudited will lower investors’ confidence because it will be the only report they receive for 364 days.


Conclusion:

For these reasons, I see having two audited reports as the only way to reduce earnings reports by 50% without diminishing the missions of the SEC, FASB, and AICPA. As flexible as the capital markets are, we must handle changes like this with care. In times of uncertainty, retail investors are often the ones who lose the most. We have to maintain an environment that supports protecting this class of investors because they ground our markets and keep bubbles from forming.
