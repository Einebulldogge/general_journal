Activist Short-sellers—Their Role and Importance to the Accounting Community (5 min)


—Written by Chris McFarland
    June 13, 2026
    Commentary last added on June 13, 2026


Introduction:

Activist short-sellers are forensic research firms that combine detailed analysis with deep skepticism to make money through shorting stocks that they believe are mispriced. They release their findings to the public, hoping to provoke a widespread negative reaction and reduce the price of a stock they have already borrowed and sold.


Background Case:

In early 2026, the activist short seller, Gotham City Research (GCR), released the latest findings of their study on Carvana, the large-cap used car dealer known for its unmatched convenience in the industry. Their report highlighted Carvana’s extensive related-party transactions and expense agreements that GCR felt were not accounted for properly in the company’s financial statements. The accuracy of their findings is still being investigated by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) after the many class-action lawsuits that have been launched against Carvana, and the car dealer has maintained their position that these claims are inaccurate and misleading.

Despite the fact that no conclusion has been reached as of yet, Carvana has seen an undeniably related decrease in its stock price since GCR’s most recent report was released. While Carvana has been a stock that is no stranger to volatility since its IPO, I believe the near 30% decrease in its stock price is evidence that traders are listening.


Different than Auditors:

Activist short-sellers have the ability to dive deeper than external auditors because they don’t have to stay within the scope of an audit. It is the auditor’s job to obtain persuasive audit evidence to form an opinion on the fairness of the financial statements that managers produce. Presently, they are not hired to expose creative accounting techniques or give an opinion as to whether a stock is overvalued or not. Now, to be clear, Gotham City Research does argue that Carvana did not properly disclose the significance of the related party transactions as it pertains to gains on loan receivables, which calls in to question if an adjustment or modification of audit opinion may have been appropriate in recent years (I do not have the expertise or ability to ascertain the merit of GCR’s claims, so I will not make an opinion on the performance of the audit). Nevertheless, the scope limitation in auditing plays a necessary role in audits, especially those of public companies.

The Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) makes the Generally Accepted Auditing Standards (GAAS) and is overseen by the SEC, whose mission has three parts: (1) protecting investors; (2) maintaining fair, orderly, and efficient markets; and (3) facilitating capital formation. Nowhere in the SEC’s mission statement does it say that they aim to help accurately price the market; that is up to the investors themselves, based on the audited financials and all of the other public information that is available. The PCAOB’s auditing standards straddle giving protection to financial statement users while not scaring away companies from going or staying public. The most evident example of this can be found in the “Going Concern assumption.” The auditor must disclose in an explanatory paragraph if they have a substantial doubt as to whether the entity under audit will remain in operation for the period of one year after the audit report is made available. Allowing the period in question to be only one year long cuts audited entities enormous slack and reduces the subjectivity involved in making this call.


Benefits of Activist Short-sellers:

Activist short-sellers fill in the gaps that the auditing industry has been designed to leave, and give managers a healthy fear of taking unfair advantage of the largely rules-based accounting system in the US. With the continued presence of GAAP fundamentals and financial ratios guiding stock valuations, creative accounting and disclosures that are just barely compliant with GAAP can undoubtedly allow investors to misprice publicly traded companies. Not to mention, non-GAAP supplementary and other information found in financial reports often receive heavy attention from external users despite the fact that auditors only perform limited procedures on these areas. Activist short-sellers understand the power that accounting still holds, even after an unqualified opinion. Many aspects of accounting are incredibly complex and run the risk of being misconstrued by the public. Activist short-sellers follow the signs, connect the dots, and communicate their findings in a way that those with a reasonable knowledge of business can better understand. In some cases, they may even act as a second line of defense to quantitative and qualitative material misstatements in financial reporting.

The best part about activist short-sellers is that the SEC rarely has to pay them. One way they do end up compensating them is through the Whistleblower Program that was created after the passing of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act. Leads that expose violations of federal securities laws are entitled to 10-30% of the money collected, which, in past years, has amounted to an annual range of $50 to $600 million. With that being said, a study from 2021 from the Journal of Accounting Research found that only about half of the reports that activist short-sellers release allege actual fraud, while 65% include accounting issues (Brendel & Ryans). For these cases, the reward will not be from the SEC; it will be entirely from the negative price movement on the targeted stock. In this way, the SEC’s allowance of short selling indirectly helps to keep financial reporting in check.


Drawbacks of Activist Short-sellers:

Ironically, activist short-sellers have faced much scrutiny from the SEC for instances of securities fraud where they spread disinformation to buy stocks at artificially depressed prices. These acts do not represent the whole of this industry, but the nature of their work will always raise doubt about the validity of any one report. An argument can be made that the headache this causes for the SEC may outweigh the benefits they receive. I argue it’s not simply about comparing the money the SEC spends on investigating activist short-sellers to the money the SEC makes from their fraud leads. The work of activist short-sellers is invaluable to exposing legally misleading financial reporting. Shining a spotlight on creative accounting may not always change the practices of managers, but it certainly gives them reason to pause and weigh the consequences of becoming the next headline on CNBC.


Conclusion:

Accounting allows a company to tell its story to the outside world. It’s a highly regulated practice, but its ties to and influence over financial decisions inevitably leave it susceptible to bending in whatever direction suits the narrator. Auditors give credibility to each story, but, following the standards of their industry, they relinquish the responsibility of consulting external users so they can do their part in maintaining the finely oiled machine that is the financial market. Activist, short-sellers represent the epitome of capitalism and its ability to self-regulate. Even as the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) works to achieve a more objectives-based accounting system, the nature of financial reporting will always leave managers opportunities to benefit from information asymmetry.

I believe the accounting community is stronger when regulators embrace managers and auditors to use professional judgment. A lack of judgment can lead to an over-reliance on the various classifications, amounts, and presentation of information found within financial reports. I also believe that activist short-sellers play an essential role in curtailing managers’ misuse of professional judgment in a very public way. These two forces acting upon the accounting community make for a more educated audience that has the ability to now use their own judgement to make more informed decisions with their capital.
